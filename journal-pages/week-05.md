## Reflective Proposal

This project builds on my earlier experiments with personal data, especially the food-based data portrait from Week 1 and the interactive visualisations from Week 2. I found the idea of “data humanism” particularly meaningful, as it allowed me to treat data not just as numbers but as something emotional and personal. Recording the tastiness of my meals showed me how everyday experiences can become meaningful data. However, my previous work mainly focused on simple representations such as coloured circles and basic interaction. For this project, I want to develop a more complex and thoughtful way of representing eating experiences over time.

The thematic focus of this project is the relationship between food, emotion, and daily life. Eating is not only a physical activity but also shaped by mood, social context, and routine. I am interested in how these factors influence how we remember and evaluate our meals. The project imagines a near-future scenario in which everyday eating experiences are continuously tracked and analysed by a personal system. Instead of only measuring nutrition or calories, this system focuses on subjective experiences such as satisfaction, mood, and social interaction.

The data for this project will be a combination of self-collected and simulated data. I will record meals over a period of time, including satisfaction level, time of day, and whether the meal was eaten alone or with others. This dataset will be relatively small but personal. I may also simulate additional data to explore patterns over a longer period. The data is not entirely objective, as it is based on personal judgement, which aligns with ideas from data humanism. The goal is not accuracy, but to reveal patterns in how eating experiences are shaped by emotional and social factors.

The visualisation will move beyond a simple grid layout. Instead of static circles, meals will be represented as dynamic elements within a timeline-based interface. The size of each element will represent satisfaction, while colour and position will reflect mood and temporal structure. Additional layers, such as a mood trend line or indicators of social context, will provide further insight. Interaction will allow users to explore different days and filter the data. The design will focus on clarity while also maintaining an expressive, human-centred aesthetic.

The provocation of this project is to question how data systems might reduce complex human experiences into measurable values. By visualising subjective data such as “how enjoyable a meal felt,” the project highlights both the possibilities and limitations of data-driven representations. I want the audience to reflect on how everyday experiences are increasingly being tracked and quantified, and to consider what might be lost in this process. Ideally, the project will encourage viewers to think more critically about how data represents their daily lives, and whether personal meaning can truly be captured through data.
